Review of Related Literature, Studies, and Systems

Summary of the conceptual, empirical, and systems-level foundations that justify the development of CLOIE.

2.1 Related Literature

The literature in Chapter 2 establishes the conceptual basis of CLOIE: outcomes should be measurable, evidence should be organized, and academic decisions should be supported by recurring stakeholder-informed review cycles.

2.1.1

Outcome-Based Education in Higher Education

OBE emphasizes measurable competencies and requires institutions to define outcomes, gather evidence, and use findings for improvement (ABET, 2024; University of the Philippines, 2025).

  • Shifts evaluation from content coverage to demonstrated attainment
  • Supports CLOIE's focus on PLO/GO/CILO monitoring
2.1.2

Learning Outcomes Assessment

Outcomes assessment requires direct and indirect evidence workflows beyond grades, including mapped indicators and attainment thresholds (Goss, 2022).

  • Assessment becomes difficult when evidence is scattered
  • CLOIE addresses this through structured consolidation
2.1.3

Quality Assurance in Higher Education

Quality assurance depends on systematic documentation, review practices, and reliable indicators rather than compliance-only routines (Javed & Alenezi, 2023; University of the Philippines, 2025).

  • Evidence organization is central to credible QA
  • CLOIE aligns with QA-oriented reporting needs
2.1.4

Continuous Quality Improvement in Academic Programs

CQI frameworks like PDCA rely on recurring data interpretation to move from reporting toward actionable enhancement (Samuel & Farrer, 2025).

  • Evidence quality determines usefulness of improvement cycles
  • CLOIE strengthens the "check" stage through consolidated outputs
2.1.5

Curriculum Relevance and Industry Alignment

Labor-market shifts require periodic recalibration of outcomes and competencies using external stakeholder insights (World Economic Forum, 2023; Mahalingam, 2024).

  • Industry-informed review can reveal hidden competency gaps
  • CLOIE integrates graduate and employer perspectives
2.1.6

Graduate Employability as Program-Relevance Indicator

Employability and tracer evidence help validate whether program outcomes remain meaningful beyond classroom completion (Sarsale et al., 2024; Reyes et al., 2025).

  • Post-graduation alignment is a practical relevance measure
  • CLOIE extends monitoring into readiness and workplace fit

2.2 Related Studies

The reviewed studies provide empirical support for CLOIE by showing the limits of fragmented workflows and the value of integrated analytics, stakeholder evidence, and centralized reporting.

2.2.1

Studies on Learning Outcomes Assessment

Research shows outcomes review remains difficult when tools and evidence are disconnected, making standardization and interpretation harder (Goss, 2022; El Marsafawy et al., 2022; Alyasin et al., 2023).

  • What was examined: outcomes assessment workflows
  • What was found: manual and scattered approaches are inefficient
  • Why it matters: CLOIE provides repeatable evidence consolidation
2.2.2

Studies on Curriculum Alignment and Industry Demands

External review, especially from industry, helps identify curriculum gaps that may not appear in internal-only evaluation (Mahalingam, 2024; World Economic Forum, 2023).

  • What was examined: curriculum relevance mechanisms
  • What was found: stakeholder participation improves alignment quality
  • Why it matters: CLOIE integrates industry and graduate evidence
2.2.3

Studies on Graduate Employability and Tracer Studies

Tracer findings reveal areas where graduate outcomes and employment alignment still diverge, informing program refinement (Sarsale et al., 2024; Reyes et al., 2025; Nabablit & Dajao, 2023).

  • What was examined: post-graduation relevance and alignment
  • What was found: employability signals can reveal outcome gaps
  • Why it matters: CLOIE links outcomes monitoring to external readiness
2.2.4

Studies on Stakeholder-Based Evaluation

No single respondent group provides a complete quality picture; multi-stakeholder evidence produces stronger interpretation (Mahalingam, 2024; Sarsale et al., 2024; Reyes et al., 2025).

  • What was examined: comparative value of stakeholder inputs
  • What was found: combined respondent perspectives improve credibility
  • Why it matters: CLOIE is designed around multi-source evaluation
2.2.5

Studies on Learning Analytics

Dashboard-driven analytics improve interpretation when they move beyond storage toward actionable patterns and priorities (Chen et al., 2023; Ramaswami et al., 2023).

  • What was examined: dashboard use and engagement effects
  • What was found: analytics is useful when it supports interpretation
  • Why it matters: CLOIE includes reporting for evidence-based decisions
2.2.6

Studies on Digital Assessment and Monitoring Platforms

Digital systems consistently improve data organization, usability, and reporting compared with manual and outdated processes (El Marsafawy et al., 2022; Nabablit & Dajao, 2023).

  • What was examined: practical platform outcomes
  • What was found: centralized systems improve monitoring quality
  • Why it matters: CLOIE operationalizes these strengths at college scope

2.3 Related Systems

The systems review demonstrates operational feasibility: institutions already use centralized platforms for outcomes documentation, analytics, and quality processes, though scope and integration differ.

2.3.1 Nuventive / Penn State Assessment Management System

What it does: centralizes learning outcomes documentation, curriculum mapping, reporting, and action planning.

Relevance to CLOIE: shows how outcomes evidence and dashboards can coexist in one continuous assessment environment, as demonstrated by Penn State Office of Planning, Assessment, and Institutional Research (2024).

2.3.2 Oklahoma State University Assessment Platform

What it does: combines Nuventive with cloud analytics for scalable reporting and high program adoption.

Relevance to CLOIE: demonstrates that centralized assessment workflows can be implemented and adopted effectively (Microsoft, 2024).

2.3.3 University of the Philippines QA Portal

What it does: integrates internal/external QA flows, evidence submission, review schedules, and multi-source survey environments.

Relevance to CLOIE: confirms that Philippine HEIs can operationalize centralized quality systems and stakeholder feedback channels, consistent with University of the Philippines (2025).

2.3.4 CHED / Regional Monitoring Systems

What it does: supports region-level digital monitoring, submission workflows, and analytics-based oversight.

Relevance to CLOIE: reinforces the policy and governance relevance of structured, transparent data workflows (Commission on Higher Education Regional Office XI, 2024, 2025).

2.3.5 Graduate Tracer / Outcomes Monitoring Systems

What it does: collects and organizes alumni evidence over time through dedicated digital platforms.

Relevance to CLOIE: supports CLOIE's goal of linking outcomes monitoring with post-graduation relevance indicators (Nabablit & Dajao, 2023).

2.4 Synthesis of Reviewed Literature, Studies, and Systems

Chapter 2 concludes that CLOIE is justified because the reviewed evidence consistently points to the need for a focused, integrated, and decision-oriented outcomes monitoring platform at college/program level.

Chapter 2 Takeaways

  • What the literature established: modern higher education expects structured evidence for quality and accreditation decisions.
  • What the studies confirmed: manual and fragmented workflows weaken interpretation, consistency, and actionability.
  • What the systems demonstrated: centralized platforms can operationalize outcomes documentation and reporting at scale.
  • What remains insufficiently addressed: a scoped platform that combines outcomes monitoring, multi-stakeholder feedback, and integrated reporting in one college-level environment.
  • Why CLOIE is justified: it targets this integration gap while remaining practical and aligned with institutional QA needs.

Chapter 2 positions CLOIE as an academic support system that improves evidence availability for interpretation and program-level decision-making, not as a replacement for academic judgment.
